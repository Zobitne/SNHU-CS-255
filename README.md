# SNHU-COMPUTER-SCIENCE
CS 255, System Analysis and Design
This is my final project for CS 255.

1. Briefly summarize the DriverPass project. Who was the client? What type of system did they want you to design?
• The DriverPass project focused on designing a system for a company that provides driving instruction and practice tests. The client, DriverPass, wanted a web based system that would allow students to register, schedule driving lessons, take online practice exams, and track their progress. They also needed administrative functionality for managing instructors, vehicles, scheduling, and reporting. My role was to gather and interpret the client’s requirements and translate them into a structured system design using UML diagrams and documentation.

2. What did you do particularly well?
• I believe I did particularly well translating business needs into clear system diagrams. My activity, sequence, and class diagrams all align directly with the requirements outlined in the business requirements document. I focused on keeping the system logical, organized, and scalable. I also made sure that the relationships between users, reservations, payments, and practice tests were clearly represented. Overall, I think my strongest area was connecting user needs to structured technical design without overcomplicating the solution.

3. If you could choose one part of your work on these documents to revise, what would you pick? How would you improve it?
• If I were to revise one part of my work, I would further refine the layout and visual clarity of some of my UML diagrams. While they are technically correct, I could improve spacing and grouping to make them even easier to read at a glance. I would also expand on edge cases, such as error handling scenarios, to strengthen the completeness of the design.

4. How did you interpret the user’s needs and implement them into your system design? Why is it so important to consider the user’s needs when designing?
• I interpreted the user’s needs by carefully reviewing the interview transcript and identifying both functional and nonfunctional requirements. I separated what the system must do, such as scheduling lessons and tracking results, from how the system must perform, such as security, reliability, and accessibility. Considering the user’s needs is critical because the system ultimately exists to solve their problems. If the design does not reflect real user workflows, even a technically correct system will fail in practice.

5. How do you approach designing software? What techniques or strategies would you use in the future to analyze and design a system?
• When I design software, I start by identifying stakeholders and clearly defining requirements. From there, I break the system into logical components and model interactions using UML diagrams. I focus on structure first before thinking about implementation details. In the future, I would continue using requirement analysis, diagram modeling, and iterative refinement. I would also incorporate more validation early in the process, such as reviewing designs with stakeholders before finalizing documentation. Clear communication and documentation are just as important as technical accuracy.
